Sonic the Hedgehog

Sonic the Hedgehog, released in 1991 for the Sega Genesis, showcased some of the most iconic pixel art of the 16-bit era. Sonic’s character design used bold colors and smooth animations to highlight his sense of speed. The pixel art style allowed for vibrant, detailed backgrounds like the lush Green Hill Zone, with layered parallax scrolling that gave the game a dynamic, immersive feel. Despite hardware limitations, Sonic’s sprite animations were incredibly fluid, helping to establish his cool, energetic personality.

Super Mario

Super Mario Bros., first released in 1985 for the NES, is one of the most influential examples of pixel art in gaming. Mario’s iconic red hat and overalls were chosen not just for color, but to help distinguish his features within the limited 8-bit resolution. The world design — from the bright blue skies to the question mark blocks — was both functional and charming, guiding players visually through the game. The clever use of color palettes and tile repetition helped Nintendo fit an expansive world into very tight memory constraints.

Pac-Man

Released in 1980, Pac-Man is a masterclass in simplicity and clarity through pixel art. The characters — including Pac-Man himself and the four ghosts — were built with minimal pixels but instantly recognizable forms. The maze layout used bright, consistent lines and bold colors to help players navigate quickly and instinctively. While limited in detail, Pac-Man’s pixel art was perfectly suited to the arcade format and small screens of the time, making it one of the most iconic visuals in video game history.
